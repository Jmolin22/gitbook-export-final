# Phone & SMS Scams (Vishing & Smishing)

#### What Are Vishing and Smishing?

**Vishing** (voice phishing) and **Smishing** (SMS phishing) are social engineering attacks delivered through **phone calls** and **text messages**.

Instead of using email, attackers exploit:

* Phone calls that sound urgent or authoritative
* Text messages with malicious links or instructions

The goal is usually to:

* Steal credentials or personal information
* Trick victims into sending money or gift cards
* Convince users to install malicious software

***

#### Why Phone and SMS Scams Are Increasing

Attackers increasingly use phone and text‑based scams because:

* People tend to trust phone calls and texts more than emails
* Phone numbers are easy to spoof
* Messages often feel more urgent or personal
* Many security tools focus primarily on email threats

These attacks work because they feel **direct, immediate, and legitimate**.

***

#### Common Vishing (Phone Call) Scenarios

Vishing attacks often involve phone calls claiming to be from:

* IT or technical support
* Banks or financial institutions
* Government agencies
* Delivery or service providers
* Company leadership or vendors

Common tactics include:

* Claiming an account is compromised
* Demanding immediate action
* Asking for verification codes or credentials
* Threatening account suspension or legal consequences

***

#### Common Smishing (Text Message) Scenarios

Smishing messages often include:

* Package delivery notifications
* Account security alerts
* Payment or refund notices
* MFA or verification requests
* “Click here immediately” language

These messages frequently contain links leading to:

* Fake login pages
* Malware downloads
* Credential‑harvesting sites

***

#### Caller ID and Message Spoofing

Attackers can easily fake or “spoof”:

* Caller ID numbers
* Organization names
* Short‑code text numbers

A call or text appearing to come from a familiar number **does not guarantee legitimacy**.

Always verify independently.

***

#### Warning Signs of Phone and SMS Scams

Be cautious if a call or text:

* Creates urgency or fear
* Demands secrecy or immediate action
* Requests passwords, codes, or payment
* Uses generic greetings
* Comes from an unexpected source

Any unexpected request for sensitive information should be treated with suspicion.

***

#### Best Practices for Phone and SMS Safety

To reduce risk from vishing and smishing attacks:

* Do not share passwords or verification codes
* Avoid clicking links in unexpected text messages
* Hang up and verify calls through official channels
* Never provide sensitive information under pressure
* Trust your instincts when something feels off

Legitimate organizations will allow time for verification.

***

#### What To Do If You Receive a Suspicious Call or Text

If you believe a call or message may be a scam:

1. Do not respond or provide information
2. Do not click any links or follow instructions
3. End the call or delete the message
4. Report the incident following your organization’s reporting process
5. Monitor accounts for unusual activity if information was shared

Reporting helps reduce risk for others.

***

#### Key Takeaways

* Phone and text scams are forms of social engineering
* Attackers exploit urgency, trust, and authority
* Caller ID and message appearance can be spoofed
* Verification is critical before taking action
* Reporting suspicious activity protects everyone
