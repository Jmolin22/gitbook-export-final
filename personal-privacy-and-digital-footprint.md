# Personal Privacy & Digital Footprint

#### What Is a Digital Footprint?

A **digital footprint** is the trail of information created by online activity. This includes:

* Social media posts and interactions
* Photos, videos, and comments
* Accounts and profiles on websites or apps
* Search activity and online purchases
* Public records or shared documents

Some of this information is created intentionally, while other parts are collected automatically.

***

#### Why Personal Privacy Matters

Information shared online can be collected, stored, and reused in ways that are difficult to control.

Overexposure of personal information can lead to:

* Identity theft
* Account compromise
* Social engineering or impersonation attacks
* Reputational harm
* Unwanted tracking or profiling

Your digital footprint shapes how others — including attackers — may view or target you.

***

#### Oversharing and Its Risks

Sharing too much information online can unintentionally create security risks.

Examples of oversharing include:

* Posting personal details such as addresses or schedules
* Sharing answers to common security questions
* Announcing travel plans in advance
* Sharing photos that reveal locations, badges, or screens

Small details can be combined to create a larger security exposure.

***

#### Social Media and Privacy Settings

Social media platforms encourage sharing, but privacy settings matter.

Best practices include:

* Reviewing privacy settings regularly
* Limiting who can view posts and personal details
* Avoiding public visibility for sensitive information
* Being cautious about friend or follower requests

Posts intended for a small audience can easily become public or permanent.

***

#### Information Attackers Look For

Attackers may use personal information to:

* Guess passwords or security question answers
* Create realistic phishing or impersonation attempts
* Build trust before launching an attack
* Identify high‑value targets

Publicly available information is often used to make scams more convincing.

***

#### Personal Devices and Online Accounts

Personal privacy is affected by how accounts and devices are managed.

Good practices include:

* Using unique passwords for each account
* Enabling multi‑factor authentication (MFA)
* Reviewing account permissions and connections
* Removing unused apps, accounts, or extensions

Unused or forgotten accounts can become security risks.

***

#### Thinking Before You Share

Before posting or sharing information online, consider:

* Who can see this?
* Could this information be misused?
* Would I be comfortable with this being permanent?
* Does this reveal more than intended?

A brief pause can prevent long‑term consequences.

***

#### What To Do If Personal Information Is Exposed

If you believe personal information has been exposed:

1. Secure affected accounts immediately
2. Change passwords and enable MFA
3. Review privacy and security settings
4. Monitor accounts for suspicious activity
5. Report the issue if organizational systems are involved

Early response helps limit potential harm.

***

#### Key Takeaways

* Digital footprints are created through everyday online activity
* Oversharing increases security and privacy risk
* Personal information is valuable to attackers
* Privacy settings and account management matter
* Awareness helps protect identity and reputation
