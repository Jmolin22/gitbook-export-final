# Physical Security & Tailgating

#### What Is Physical Security?

**Physical security** involves protecting buildings, rooms, devices, and physical assets from unauthorized access.

Even with strong digital controls, physical access to systems or workspaces can allow attackers to:

* Steal devices or data
* Install malware
* Access sensitive information
* Bypass technical security controls

Physical security is a critical but often overlooked part of cybersecurity.

***

#### What Is Tailgating?

**Tailgating** occurs when an unauthorized person gains access to a secured area by following someone who is authorized to enter.

Common examples include:

* Holding a secured door open for someone without a badge
* Allowing someone to follow you into a restricted space
* Letting a “visitor” enter without proper verification

Tailgating is a form of social engineering that relies on politeness and trust.

***

#### Why Physical Security Attacks Are Effective

Physical security attacks succeed because people tend to:

* Avoid confrontation
* Assume others are authorized
* Feel pressured to be polite or helpful
* Trust uniforms, badges, or confident behavior

Attackers exploit these instincts to bypass access controls.

***

#### Common Physical Security Risks

Physical security incidents may involve:

* Unauthorized individuals in secured areas
* Unattended or unlocked workstations
* Stolen or lost devices
* Visible sensitive information on desks or screens
* Shared or propped‑open doors

These risks increase when environments are busy or distracting.

***

#### Protecting Secured Areas

Best practices for secured spaces include:

* Wearing visible identification or badges when required
* Not allowing others to enter behind you without proper access
* Challenging or reporting unfamiliar individuals appropriately
* Keeping doors and access points closed and secured

Security controls are only effective when consistently followed.

***

#### Workstation and Desk Security

Unattended workstations can expose sensitive information.

Good practices include:

* Locking screens when stepping away
* Logging out when finishing work
* Keeping sensitive documents secured
* Following clean desk guidelines where applicable

Physical access to a logged‑in workstation can lead to significant compromise.

***

#### Visitors and Contractors

Visitors and contractors should be:

* Properly signed in
* Clearly identified
* Escorted when required
* Restricted from sensitive areas

Unauthorized access is often gained by blending in as a “normal visitor.”

***

#### How to Respond to Physical Security Concerns

If you notice a potential physical security issue:

1. Do not put yourself in danger
2. Follow established reporting procedures
3. Notify security or appropriate staff
4. Provide details such as location and description

Reporting concerns helps maintain a secure environment.

***

#### Key Takeaways

* Cybersecurity includes physical security
* Tailgating is a common and effective attack method
* Politeness can be exploited by attackers
* Locked screens and secured spaces reduce risk
* Reporting concerns helps protect everyone
