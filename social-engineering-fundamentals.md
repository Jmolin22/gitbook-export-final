# Social Engineering Fundamentals

#### What Is Social Engineering?

**Social engineering** is a type of attack that relies on **manipulating people**, rather than breaking into systems.

Instead of exploiting software vulnerabilities, attackers exploit:

* Trust
* Emotion
* Authority
* Urgency
* Curiosity

Social engineering is involved in **many types of cyberattacks**, including phishing, impersonation, phone scams, and credential theft.

***

#### Why Social Engineering Is So Effective

Social engineering works because it targets **human behavior**, not technology.

Even experienced and knowledgeable users can be tricked because attackers:

* Create believable scenarios
* Apply pressure to act quickly
* Pretend to be trusted individuals or organizations
* Exploit normal workplace routines

Falling for a social engineering attempt is not a sign of carelessness — it’s a sign that the attack was well designed.

***

#### Common Social Engineering Tactics

Attackers frequently use the following techniques:

* **Urgency**\
  “This needs to be done immediately.”
* **Authority**\
  Pretending to be IT, management, or a trusted vendor
* **Fear**\
  Threats of account suspension or security incidents
* **Curiosity**\
  Teasing valuable or interesting information
* **Helpfulness**\
  Asking for “quick assistance” or a small favor

These tactics are often layered together.

***

#### Common Types of Social Engineering Attacks

Social engineering appears in many forms, including:

* Phishing emails
* Fake login pages
* Phone scams (vishing)
* Text message scams (smishing)
* Impersonation of coworkers or vendors
* Fake technical support or security alerts

The delivery method may change, but the underlying manipulation is the same.

***

#### Why Technical Controls Aren’t Enough

Security tools like:

* Firewalls
* Antivirus software
* Email filters
* MFA

Provide important protection — but **they cannot stop every social engineering attempt**.

Human awareness and decision‑making are critical last lines of defense.

***

#### Social Engineering and Trust

Attackers often exploit:

* Familiar names or branding
* Real‑world events
* Previously compromised information
* Publicly available details from social media or websites

The goal is to appear _legitimate enough_ that their request doesn’t raise suspicion.

***

#### How to Protect Yourself from Social Engineering

The most effective defenses include:

* Slowing down when requests feel urgent
* Verifying requests through a second channel
* Being cautious with unexpected messages or calls
* Questioning requests for credentials or sensitive information
* Reporting suspicious interactions promptly

Trust should be **verified**, not assumed.

***

#### What To Do If You Suspect Social Engineering

If something feels suspicious:

1. Do not provide information or take action
2. Do not click links or open attachments
3. Verify the request independently
4. Report the interaction using your organization’s reporting process

Early reporting helps protect others.

***

#### Key Takeaways

* Social engineering targets people, not systems
* Anyone can be affected, regardless of experience
* Attackers rely on urgency, authority, and trust
* Awareness and verification reduce risk
* Reporting suspicious activity is critical
