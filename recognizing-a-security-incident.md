# Recognizing a Security Incident

#### What Is a Security Incident?

A **security incident** is any event that threatens the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of systems, accounts, or data.

Security incidents do not always involve obvious or immediate damage. Many incidents begin subtly and worsen over time if not identified and reported.

Recognizing potential incidents early helps minimize impact.

***

#### Why Early Recognition Matters

Early detection of security incidents can:

* Reduce the scope of damage
* Prevent further compromise
* Protect sensitive data and systems
* Enable faster response and recovery

Many major incidents start as **small warning signs** that are overlooked or dismissed.

***

#### Common Signs of a Security Incident

Security incidents can present in many ways, including:

* Unrecognized login notifications or alerts
* Unexpected password resets or MFA prompts
* Emails sent from your account that you didn’t send
* Files missing, altered, or encrypted
* Programs or pop‑ups appearing unexpectedly
* Devices behaving unusually or slowing down suddenly

Any unusual behavior should be taken seriously.

***

#### Account‑Related Warning Signs

Account compromise is one of the most common security issues.

Watch for:

* Failed login notifications you didn’t initiate
* Suspicious account activity or changes
* Unauthorized access to email or cloud services
* Requests for verification codes you did not request

Unexpected account changes often indicate attempted or successful compromise.

***

#### Device‑Related Warning Signs

Devices may show signs of compromise through:

* Unexpected software installations
* Disabled security tools
* Frequent crashes or abnormal behavior
* Unauthorized remote access
* Difficulty accessing files or applications

If something feels “off,” it’s worth investigating.

***

#### Email, Messaging, and Communication Indicators

Security incidents may also involve communication systems.

Examples include:

* Messages appearing sent from your account without your knowledge
* Suspicious replies to emails you didn’t send
* Coworkers reporting unusual messages from you
* Unexpected attachments or links being shared automatically

These symptoms may indicate account misuse.

***

#### Not All Incidents Look the Same

Security incidents may involve:

* Accidental data exposure
* Stolen or lost devices
* Malware infections
* Credential compromise
* Policy violations
* Physical security breaches

You do not need to know _what_ happened — only that something unusual occurred.

***

#### What If You’re Unsure?

Uncertainty should not stop reporting.

If you are unsure whether something is a security incident:

* Treat it as suspicious
* Ask questions
* Report it for review

It is better to report a false alarm than to miss a real incident.

***

#### What To Do When You Suspect an Incident

If you believe a security incident may be occurring:

1. Stop further activity if safe to do so
2. Do not attempt to investigate deeply on your own
3. Preserve evidence if possible (do not delete messages or files)
4. Report the incident using your organization’s reporting process

Prompt reporting allows trained staff to respond appropriately.

***

#### Key Takeaways

* Security incidents often start with small warning signs
* Unusual account or device behavior is a red flag
* Early recognition reduces damage
* You do not need proof to report concerns
* Reporting helps protect systems and people
