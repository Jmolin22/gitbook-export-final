# How to Report Security Concerns

#### Why Reporting Matters

Timely reporting of security concerns helps protect individuals, systems, and organizations from further harm.

Many security incidents:

* Start small
* Grow if not addressed
* Affect more than one person or system

Reporting concerns early allows security teams to respond quickly and reduce impact.

***

#### What Should Be Reported?

You should report **anything that seems unusual or suspicious**, even if you’re not sure it’s a security incident.

Examples include:

* Suspected phishing, vishing, or smishing messages
* Unexpected MFA prompts
* Unusual account or device behavior
* Lost or stolen devices
* Possible data exposure
* Suspicious phone calls, emails, or messages
* Physical security concerns or tailgating

If something doesn’t feel right, it’s worth reporting.

***

#### You Don’t Need Proof

You do **not** need to investigate or confirm an incident before reporting it.

Security teams prefer:

* Early alerts
* Partial information
* Questions or uncertainty

False alarms are expected and acceptable — missed incidents are far more costly.

***

#### How to Report a Security Concern

Follow your organization’s established reporting process. This may include:

* Using a designated security or IT email address
* Submitting a ticket or report through a form or portal
* Contacting the SOC or IT help desk
* Notifying a supervisor or instructor if applicable

If you’re unsure how to report, ask — getting guidance is better than staying silent.

***

#### What Information to Include

When reporting a concern, include as much relevant information as you can, such as:

* What happened or what you noticed
* When it occurred
* How you discovered it
* Any messages, emails, or links involved
* Screenshots or attachments (if safe to provide)

Do not delay reporting just to gather more details.

***

#### What NOT to Do

When a security concern arises, avoid:

* Deleting suspicious emails or messages before reporting
* Clicking links or interacting further
* Attempting to fix the issue yourself
* Sharing details widely before reporting

Preserving information helps security teams investigate effectively.

***

#### What Happens After You Report?

After a report is submitted:

* Security staff review and assess the situation
* Additional information may be requested
* Appropriate response actions are taken
* Broader notices may be issued if needed

Reporting is intended to help — **not to assign blame**.

***

#### Reporting Is Everyone’s Responsibility

Security teams, tools, and monitoring systems are important, but they cannot see everything.

Individuals play a critical role by:

* Staying aware
* Speaking up
* Reporting concerns promptly

Strong security depends on shared responsibility.

***

#### Key Takeaways

* Early reporting reduces damage and risk
* You do not need certainty to report a concern
* Suspicious emails, calls, texts, and behavior should be reported
* Preserve information when possible
* Reporting helps protect everyone
