# Impersonation & Authority Attacks

#### What Are Impersonation and Authority Attacks?

**Impersonation and authority attacks** are social engineering techniques where attackers pretend to be someone trusted or powerful in order to influence behavior.

Attackers commonly impersonate:

* IT or technical support
* Managers or executives
* Vendors or business partners
* Financial institutions
* Government agencies

The goal is to use **perceived authority or trust** to bypass skepticism and normal security checks.

***

#### Why Authority-Based Attacks Are Effective

People are naturally conditioned to:

* Trust authority figures
* Respond quickly to leadership requests
* Avoid questioning instructions from “official” sources

Attackers exploit this by:

* Creating pressure to act immediately
* Claiming consequences for delay
* Making the request sound routine or confidential

These attacks succeed because they feel **legitimate and time‑sensitive**, not because users are careless.

***

#### Common Impersonation Scenarios

Impersonation attacks frequently involve scenarios such as:

* “IT support” requesting password resets or codes
* A “manager” asking for urgent assistance
* A “vendor” requesting payment or account changes
* A “bank” or “government” agency requesting verification
* An “executive” asking for secrecy or fast action

The sender, caller, or message may appear highly convincing.

***

#### Business Email and Internal Impersonation

Internal-looking impersonation attacks can be especially dangerous.

Common examples include:

* Emails that appear to come from leadership
* Requests for gift cards, payments, or wire transfers
* Changes to payment or account details
* Instructions sent during busy or stressful times

These attacks rely on familiarity and trust within an organization.

***

#### Red Flags of Impersonation Attempts

Be cautious if a request:

* Emphasizes urgency or secrecy
* Asks you to bypass normal procedures
* Requests credentials, codes, or payment
* Comes from an unexpected channel
* Feels “off,” even if it looks legitimate

Pressure to act quickly is often intentional.

***

#### Verification Is Not Disrespectful

One of the most important defenses against impersonation attacks is **verification**.

Verification may include:

* Confirming requests through a known phone number
* Using a different communication method
* Checking with a supervisor or colleague
* Following established processes

Legitimate leaders and organizations expect verification — **attackers do not**.

***

#### Best Practices to Prevent Impersonation Attacks

To reduce the risk of impersonation and authority attacks:

* Pause when requests feel urgent or unusual
* Verify identity before acting on sensitive requests
* Follow established approval and escalation processes
* Be cautious with unexpected financial or account requests
* Never share credentials or verification codes

Security procedures exist to protect everyone, including leadership.

***

#### What To Do If You Suspect an Impersonation Attempt

If you believe someone may be impersonating a trusted individual or organization:

1. Do not comply with the request
2. Stop the interaction if possible
3. Verify the request independently
4. Report the incident according to your organization’s process
5. Preserve messages or call details if safe to do so

Early reporting can prevent larger incidents.

***

#### Key Takeaways

* Impersonation attacks rely on perceived trust and authority
* Attackers often pressure victims to act quickly or secretly
* Verification is a critical defense, not a challenge to authority
* Normal procedures help prevent fraud and compromise
* Reporting suspicious requests protects others
