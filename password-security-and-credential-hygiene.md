# Password Security & Credential Hygiene

#### What Are Credentials?

**Credentials** are the pieces of information used to prove your identity when signing in to an account. The most common credentials are:

* Usernames
* Passwords
* PINs
* Security questions
* One‑time codes (when used with MFA)

Protecting your credentials is one of the most important steps you can take to protect your accounts and personal information.

***

#### Why Password Security Matters

Stolen or reused passwords are one of the **leading causes of account compromise**. Attackers often obtain passwords through phishing, data breaches, or malware and then attempt to reuse them across many services.

A single weak or reused password can expose:

* Email accounts
* School or work systems
* Financial accounts
* Cloud storage and personal data

***

#### Common Password Mistakes

Many security incidents are caused by everyday habits, including:

* Reusing the same password on multiple sites
* Choosing short or predictable passwords
* Storing passwords in unsecured notes or documents
* Sharing passwords with others
* Using passwords found in previous data breaches

Even “complex‑looking” passwords are unsafe if they are reused or compromised.

***

#### Best Practices for Strong Passwords

Follow these guidelines to create and maintain secure passwords:

* Use **long passphrases** instead of short passwords\
  (Example: a sentence or combination of unrelated words)
* Avoid personal information such as:
  * Names
  * Birthdates
  * Addresses
* Never reuse passwords across different accounts
* Change passwords immediately if an account is suspected to be compromised

Longer passwords are generally **more secure than short, complex ones**.

***

#### Using Password Managers

A **password manager** is a recommended tool that:

* Generates strong, unique passwords for every site
* Stores passwords securely in an encrypted vault
* Reduces the need to remember multiple passwords

Password managers significantly reduce the risk of credential reuse and make strong password habits easier to maintain.

***

#### Credential Reuse and Why It’s Dangerous

When attackers obtain credentials from one breach, they often attempt **credential stuffing**, which means:

* Automatically trying the same username/password across many services
* Exploiting users who reuse credentials

This is why **one compromised password can lead to multiple account takeovers**.

***

#### Sharing Passwords: What Not to Do

Passwords should **never** be shared via:

* Email
* Text messages
* Chat applications
* Phone calls

Legitimate organizations will **not** ask you to share your password.

***

#### What To Do If a Password Is Compromised

If you believe your password has been exposed:

1. Change the password immediately
2. Update any other accounts using the same password
3. Enable Multi‑Factor Authentication (MFA) if available
4. Report the incident following your organization’s reporting process

Quick action can prevent further damage.

***

#### Key Takeaways

* Passwords are a primary target for attackers
* Reusing passwords dramatically increases risk
* Long, unique passphrases are more secure
* Password managers make safe password habits easier
* Protecting credentials protects _everything_ tied to them
