# Multi‑Factor Authentication (MFA)

#### What Is Multi‑Factor Authentication?

**Multi‑Factor Authentication (MFA)** is a security method that requires **more than one form of verification** to sign in to an account.

Instead of relying on _only_ a password, MFA combines at least two of the following:

* **Something you know** – like a password or PIN
* **Something you have** – such as a phone, app, or hardware token
* **Something you are** – biometric data like a fingerprint or face recognition

This layered approach makes it much harder for attackers to access accounts.

***

#### Why MFA Is Important

Passwords alone are no longer sufficient protection. Even strong passwords can be:

* Phished
* Reused from previous data breaches
* Stolen by malware

MFA provides a **critical second barrier**.\
Even if an attacker steals a password, MFA can block them from signing in.

In many cases, MFA can stop **the majority of account compromise attempts**.

***

#### Common Types of MFA

There are several common MFA methods you may encounter:

* **Authentication apps** (recommended)\
  Generate time‑based codes on your device
* **Push notifications**\
  Approve sign‑in attempts on a trusted device
* **Text message (SMS) codes**\
  Less secure, but better than no MFA
* **Hardware security keys**\
  Physical devices that must be present to sign in
* **Biometrics**\
  Fingerprints or facial recognition on supported devices

Not all MFA methods provide the same level of protection, but **any MFA is better than none**.

***

#### MFA Fatigue and Push Notification Attacks

Attackers sometimes attempt **MFA fatigue attacks** by repeatedly sending push notifications, hoping the user will approve one out of confusion or frustration.

Warning signs include:

* Repeated MFA prompts you did not initiate
* Push notifications arriving unexpectedly
* Login requests at unusual times

***

#### What To Do If You Receive Unexpected MFA Prompts

If you receive an MFA request you did not initiate:

1. **Do not approve the request**
2. Change your password immediately
3. Report the incident according to your organization’s reporting process
4. Review recent account activity if available

Approving a single malicious MFA request can give an attacker full access to your account.

***

#### Best Practices for MFA

To use MFA safely and effectively:

* Enable MFA on **all available accounts**, especially:
  * Email
  * Cloud services
  * Financial accounts
* Use an authenticator app instead of SMS when possible
* Never approve MFA requests you did not start
* Keep backup recovery options secure and up to date

MFA works best when combined with strong password practices.

***

#### MFA Is Not Optional for High‑Value Accounts

Accounts that control or access sensitive systems require extra protection, including:

* Administrator accounts
* Email accounts
* Learning management systems
* Financial or payment systems

MFA is considered a **baseline security requirement** for these accounts.

***

#### Key Takeaways

* MFA adds a critical second layer of security
* Passwords alone are no longer enough
* MFA can stop attackers even after passwords are stolen
* Unexpected MFA prompts are a warning sign
* Approving an unrecognized request can compromise an account
